Project:
Parental-Control with Modern Tech


My vision is for a future where children can go anywhere on the internet because any questionable and inapropriate content, including comments and images will be redacted, and I think this scenario is possible using current technologies. Your child’s ability to get online is essential and it’s important to find a real solution to this problem.

Parental-control apps have never been able to beat motivated preteens and while most can recognize that children aren’t mature enough to navigate the internet unsupervised, it’s unsettling how intrusive parental-control software really is. That parents feel the need to use these tools proves to me that there is an important and interesting opportunity for a new product to enter this crowded market. With machine learning tools, data scrapers, and distributed computing, technology can finally strike a balance between allowing children freedom to wander the internet while protecting them from inappropriate content through dynamically redacting text, images, and videos.

My motivation is primarily drawn from current events and family. For years my sister has been frustrated with the way her son uses his superior computer knowledge to undermine his parents. She has found and closed multiple paypal, email and facebook accounts over the years starting from when he was six. Recently there has been a lot of media attention focusing on cyberbullying, online hate speech, disinformation and toxic tribalism. Governments have severly critisized Facebook for years over their privacy policy and disinformation and now even more worrysome are child predaters infiltrating the comment sections on YouTube and eveny creating content.

A quick google search will find a few parental-control apps come to the top like in this article from PCMAG (Rubenking & Moore , 2019). I’ve chosen Qustodio as my benchmark. If you venture to the comment section of many YouTube reviews of this product (link1 link2 link3), you’ll notice that on the one hand children find this app too restrictive and they actively share information, or circumvent how to disable or circumvent it while on the other hand parents are singing it’s praises about the control they can exert over their childrens internet use. When you see the extent to which children dislike this experience and when you know how easy it is to get around this program, it should be obvious that there needs to be a better option.

Imagine creating a standard Windows account for your child, then logging in and executing a launcher that will install a virtual machine taking you to a short set up page. As the child logs on to the account, the virtual machine will immidiately launch and greet the child with Linux and an entire suite of tools to explore. As the child navigates through the internet they are completely free to go anywhere but may not be able to view certain text, images or videos while on a webpage. Social media websites are now available to visit, but they might not be able to view all the content, participate in certain discussions or sign up with the wrong age.

In this scenario the limits are more subtle, and you aren’t hiding from anyone; no parent has any reason to look over your shoulder. Internet speeds are always increasing and more threads are always being added to processors to where we have extra margin to use and we can now simulate a great user experience that can satisfy every child’s curiosity while keeping them safe.

The last point I’ll touch on has to do with fostering a good relationship with your child. It’s well documented as this article form wiley.com (Hawk, S., Becht, A. and Branje, S.) shows that snooping on a child has a negative effect on the family dynamic and studies show it can lead to a breakdown in communication and can leave a lasting impression on the psyche of a child. Reading comments from a reddit post that I found here, you can clearly see that the intended effect of this software has been lost and the conversations have turned to invasion of privacy. Some are even looking for laws that can protect their privacy. There is a complete collapse of trust between these teens and their parents. My research so far has suggested that child care professionals, parents and caregivers would all agree that it is important to develope a good relationship with your child not just for your own sake, but for the healthy development of your child. Paraphrased from acpeds.org (Veritas, D.).

In a perfect world, the install package would include a virtual machine that could record snapshot backups. The image would be a forked distro of Debian focused on the freedom to go anywhere and the program would be baked into the distro. During the installation process, there could be a short questionnaire for filtering-based pre-sets. The application would run between the network card and the browser and a lightweight crawler would be used to speed up browsing. The system would redact inappropriate content before displaying the page. This would allow every page on every site to be viewable. CXXNET (Information found on Butler Analytics.) deep learning tool could flag suspicious content and anything not recognized could be sent to AWS to be tested against similar content in a database and redacted if necessary.

The obstacles would be relying on an open source community which could lead to delays and the cost of developers write and maintain the distro and software and push security patches and updates. The up front cost could be large as we would need to rent space with AWS so we can build our machine learning. We would need a programmer for the crawler and we would have to license a virtual machine from a company like VMWare. The annual price might be too high for what the consumer gets. This also doesn’t address mobile devices, which make up a significant percentage of the market. There are also challenges this type of software faces. The software needs to be self-explanatory on why it’s useful to the child.

This software has to walk the fine line between removing content while not feeling overbearing, because there is nothing you can do to your PC that can stop a child from installing Linux on to a thumb drive, popping the CMOS battery if you protected the BIOS, booting off the USB and installing a VPN which would leave them unprotected.
